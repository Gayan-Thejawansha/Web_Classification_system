   Gautama Buddha, also known as Siddhrtha Gautama, Shakyamuni
   Buddha,^[125][note 3] or simply the Buddha, after the title of
   [126]Buddha, was an ascetic ([127][ramaGa) and [128]sage,^[129][3] on
   whose teachings [130]Buddhism was founded.^[131][web 2] He is believed
   to have lived and taught mostly in the eastern part of [132]Ancient
   India sometime between the sixth and fourth centuries
   [133]BCE.^[134][4]^[135][note 4]

   Gautama taught a [136]Middle Way between sensual indulgence and the
   severe [137]asceticism found in the [138][ramaGa movement^[139][5]
   common in his region. He later taught throughout other regions of
   eastern India such as [140]Magadha and [141]Kosala.^[142][4]^[143][6]

   Gautama is the primary figure in Buddhism. He is recognized by
   Buddhists as an [144]enlightened or divine^[145][7] teacher who
   attained full [146]Buddhahood, and shared his insights to help
   [147]sentient beings end rebirth and [148]suffering. Accounts of his
   life, discourses, and [149]monastic rules are believed by Buddhists to
   have been summarized after his death and memorized by his followers.
   Various collections of teachings attributed to him were passed down by
   [150]oral tradition and [151]first committed to writing about 400 years
   later.

Historical Siddhrtha Gautama

   Ancient kingdoms and cities of India during the time of the Buddha.

   Scholars are hesitant to make unqualified claims about the historical
   facts of the Buddha's life. Most accept that he lived, taught and
   founded a monastic order during the [181]Mahajanapada era during the
   reign of [182]Bimbisara (c. 558  c. 491 BCE),^[183][8]^[184][9] the
   ruler of the [185]Magadha empire, and died during the early years of
   the reign of [186]Ajasattu, who was the successor of Bimbisara, thus
   making him a younger contemporary of [187]Mahavira, the Jain
   [188]tirthankara.^[189][10]^[190][11] Apart from the [191]Vedic
   [192]Brahmins, the Buddha's lifetime coincided with the flourishing of
   influential [193]ZramaGa schools of thought like [194]j+vika,
   [195]Crvka, [196]Jainism, and [197]Ajana.^[198][12] [199]Brahmajala
   Sutta records sixty-two such schools of thought. It was also the age of
   influential thinkers like [200]Mahavira (referred to as 'Nigantha
   Nataputta' in Pali Canon)^[201][13][202]), [203]PkraGa Kassapa,
   [204]Makkhali Gosla, [205]Ajita Kesakambal+, [206]Pakudha Kaccyana, and
   [207]Sajaya Belammhaputta, as recorded in [208]Samaaphala Sutta, whose
   viewpoints the Buddha most certainly must have been acquainted
   with.^[209][14]^[210][15]^[211][note 5] Indeed, [212]Sariputta and
   [213]Moggallna, two of the foremost disciples of the Buddha, were
   formerly the foremost disciples of Sajaya Belammhaputta, the
   skeptic;^[214][16] and the Pali canon frequently depicts Buddha
   engaging in debate with the adherents of rival schools of thoughts.
   Thus, Buddha was just one of the many [ramaGa philosophers of that
   time.^[215][17] There is also evidence to suggest that the two masters,
   [216]Alara Kalama and [217]Uddaka Ramaputta, were indeed historical
   figures and they most probably taught Buddha two different forms of
   meditative techniques.^[218][18] While the general sequence of "birth,
   maturity, renunciation, search, awakening and liberation, teaching,
   death" is widely accepted,^[219][19]^[[220]page needed] there is less
   consensus on the veracity of many details contained in traditional
   biographies.^[221][20]^[222][21]

   The times of Gautama's birth and death are uncertain. Most historians
   in the early 20th century dated his lifetime as circa 563 BCE to 483
   BCE.^[223][1]^[224][22] More recently his death is dated later, between
   411 and 400 BCE, while at a symposium on this question held in
   1988,^[225][23]^[226][24]^[227][25] the majority of those who presented
   definite opinions gave dates within 20 years either side of 400 BCE for
   the Buddha's death.^[228][1]^[229][26]^[230][note 4] These alternative
   chronologies, however, have not yet been accepted by all
   historians.^[231][31]^[232][32]^[233][note 6]

   The evidence of the early texts suggests that Siddhrtha Gautama was
   born into the [234]Shakya clan, a community that was on the periphery,
   both geographically and culturally, of the eastern Indian subcontinent
   in the 5th century BCE.^[235][34] It was either a small republic, or an
   [236]oligarchy, and his father was an elected chieftain, or
   oligarch.^[237][34] According to the Buddhist tradition, Gautama was
   born in [238]Lumbini, now in modern-day [239]Nepal, and raised in the
   Shakya capital of Kapilvastu, which may have been either in what is
   present day [240]Tilaurakot, Nepal or [241]Piprahwa, India.^[242][note
   1] He obtained his enlightenment in [243]Bodh Gaya, gave his first
   sermon in [244]Sarnath, and died in [245]Kushinagar.

   No written records about Gautama were found from his lifetime or some
   centuries thereafter. One [246]Edict of Asoka, who reigned from circa
   269 BCE to 232 BCE, commemorates the Emperor's pilgrimage to the
   Buddha's birthplace in [247]Lumbini. Another one of his edicts mentions
   the titles of several [248]Dhamma texts, establishing the existence of
   a written Buddhist tradition at least by the time of the [249]Maurya
   era. These texts may be the precursor of the [250]Pli
   Canon.^[251][48]^[252][web 10] ^[253][note 7] The oldest surviving
   Buddhist manuscripts are the [254]Gandhran Buddhist texts, reported to
   have been found in or around [255]Ha a near [256]Jalalabad in eastern
   [257]Afghanistan and now preserved in the [258]British Library. They
   are written in the [259]Gndhr+ language using the [260]Kharosthi script
   on twenty-seven [261]birch bark manuscripts and date from the first
   century BCE to the third century CE.^[262][web 11]

Traditional biographies

   Buddha by [263]Otgonbayar Ershuu

Biographical sources

   The sources for the life of Siddhrtha Gautama are a variety of
   different, and sometimes conflicting, traditional biographies. These
   include the [264]Buddhacarita, [265]Lalitavistara Sktra, [266]Mahvastu,
   and the Nidnakath.^[267][49] Of these, the
   Buddhacarita^[268][50]^[269][51]^[270][52] is the earliest full
   biography, an epic poem written by the poet [271]A[vaghoca in the first
   century CE.^[272][53] The Lalitavistara Sktra is the next oldest
   biography, a [273]Mahyna/[274]Sarvstivda biography dating to the 3rd
   century CE.^[275][54] The Mahvastu from the [276]MahsCghika
   [277]Lokottaravda tradition is another major biography, composed
   incrementally until perhaps the 4th century CE.^[278][54] The
   [279]Dharmaguptaka biography of the Buddha is the most exhaustive, and
   is entitled the AbhinickramaGa Sktra,^[280][55] and various Chinese
   translations of this date between the 3rd and 6th century CE. The
   Nidnakath is from the [281]Theravada tradition in [282]Sri Lanka and
   was composed in the 5th century by [283]Buddhaghoca.^[284][56]

   From canonical sources, the [285]Jataka tales, the Mahapadana Sutta (DN
   14), and the Achariyabhuta Sutta (MN 123) which include selective
   accounts that may be older, but are not full biographies. The Jtakas
   retell previous lives of Gautama as a [286]bodhisattva, and the first
   collection of these can be dated among the earliest Buddhist
   texts.^[287][57] The Mahpadna Sutta and Achariyabhuta Sutta both
   recount miraculous events surrounding Gautama's birth, such as the
   bodhisattva's descent from the [288]Tucita Heaven into his mother's
   womb.

Nature of traditional depictions

   [289]My miraculously giving birth to Siddhrtha. [290]Sanskrit,
   [291]palm-leaf manuscript. [292]Nland, [293]Bihar, India. [294]Pla
   period

   In the earliest Buddhists texts, the [295]nikyas and [296]gamas, the
   Buddha is not depicted as possessing [297]omniscience
   (sabbau)^[298][58] nor is he depicted as being an eternal transcendent
   (lokottara) being. According to [299]Bhikkhu Analayo, ideas of the
   Buddha's omniscience (along with an increasing tendency to deify him
   and his biography) are found only later, in the [300]Mahayana sutras
   and later [301]Pali commentaries or texts such as the
   Mahvastu.^[302][58] In the Sandaka Sutta, the Buddha's disciple Ananda
   outlines an argument against the claims of teachers who say they are
   all knowing ^[303][web 12] while in the Tevijjavacchagotta Sutta the
   Buddha himself states that he has never made a claim to being
   omniscient, instead he claimed to have the "higher knowledges"
   ([304]abhij).^[305][59] The earliest biographical material from the
   Pali Nikayas focuses on the Buddha's life as a [306][ramaGa, his search
   for enlightenment under various teachers such as [307]Alara Kalama and
   his forty five year career as a teacher.^[308][web 13]

   Traditional biographies of Gautama generally include numerous miracles,
   omens, and supernatural events. The character of the Buddha in these
   traditional biographies is often that of a fully transcendent (Skt.
   lokottara) and perfected being who is unencumbered by the mundane
   world. In the Mahvastu, over the course of many lives, Gautama is said
   to have developed supra-mundane abilities including: a painless birth
   conceived without intercourse; no need for sleep, food, medicine, or
   bathing, although engaging in such "in conformity with the world";
   omniscience, and the ability to "suppress karma".^[309][60]
   Nevertheless, some of the more ordinary details of his life have been
   gathered from these traditional sources. In modern times there has been
   an attempt to form a [310]secular understanding of Siddhrtha Gautama's
   life by omitting the traditional supernatural elements of his early
   biographies.

   Andrew Skilton writes that the Buddha was never historically regarded
   by Buddhist traditions as being merely human:

     It is important to stress that, despite modern Theravada teachings
     to the contrary (often a sop to skeptical Western pupils), he was
     never seen as being merely human. For instance, he is often
     described as having the thirty-two major and eighty minor marks or
     signs of a mahpuruca, "superman"; the Buddha himself denied that he
     was either a man or a [311]god; and in the [312]Mahparinibbna Sutta
     he states that he could live for an [313]aeon were he asked to do
     so.^[314][61]

   The ancient Indians were generally unconcerned with chronologies, being
   more focused on philosophy. Buddhist texts reflect this tendency,
   providing a clearer picture of what Gautama may have taught than of the
   dates of the events in his life. These texts contain descriptions of
   the culture and daily life of ancient India which can be corroborated
   from the [315]Jain scriptures, and make the Buddha's time the earliest
   period in [316]Indian history for which significant accounts
   exist.^[317][62] British author [318]Karen Armstrong writes that
   although there is very little information that can be considered
   historically sound, we can be reasonably confident that Siddhrtha
   Gautama did exist as a historical figure.^[319][63] Michael Carrithers
   goes a bit further by stating that the most general outline of "birth,
   maturity, renunciation, search, awakening and liberation, teaching,
   death" must be true.^[320][19]

Biography

Conception and birth

   Maya's dream of the Birth of Gautama Siddharta
   Purported birthplace of Gautama Buddha in [321]Lumbini,
   [322]Nepal,^[323][note 1] a holy shrine also for many
   non-Buddhists.^[324][note 8]

   The Buddhist tradition regards [325]Lumbini, in present-day [326]Nepal
   to be the birthplace of the Buddha.^[327][64]^[328][note 1] He grew up
   in [329]Kapilavastu.^[330][note 1] The exact site of ancient
   Kapilavastu is unknown.^[331][65] It may have been either
   [332]Piprahwa, [333]Uttar Pradesh, present-day India,^[334][43] or
   [335]Tilaurakot, present-day [336]Nepal.^[337][66] Both places belonged
   to the Sakya territory, and are located only 15 miles apart.^[338][66]

   Gautama was born as a [339]Kshatriya,^[340][67]^[341][note 9] the son
   of [342]Zuddhodana, "an elected chief of the [343]Shakya
   clan",^[344][4] whose capital was Kapilavastu, and who were later
   annexed by the growing Kingdom of [345]Kosala during the Buddha's
   lifetime. Gautama was the [346]family name. His mother, [347]Maya
   (Mydev+), Suddhodana's wife, was a [348]Koliyan princess. Legend has it
   that, on the night Siddhartha was conceived, Queen Maya dreamt that a
   [349]white elephant with six white tusks entered her right
   side,^[350][69]^[351][70] and ten [352]months later^[353][71]
   Siddhartha was born. As was the Shakya tradition, when his mother Queen
   Maya became pregnant, she left Kapilvastu for her father's kingdom to
   give birth. However, her son is said to have been born on the way, at
   Lumbini, in a garden beneath a [354]sal tree.

   The day of the Buddha's birth is widely celebrated in Theravada
   countries as [355]Vesak.^[356][72] [357]Buddha's Birthday is called
   Buddha [358]Purnima in Nepal, Bangladesh, and India as he is believed
   to have been born on a full moon day. Various sources hold that the
   Buddha's mother died at his birth, a few days or seven days later. The
   infant was given the name Siddhartha (Pli: Siddhattha), meaning "he who
   achieves his aim". During the birth celebrations, the hermit seer
   [359]Asita journeyed from his mountain abode and announced that the
   child would either become a great king ([360]chakravartin) or a great
   [361]sadhu.^[362][73] By traditional account,^[[363]which?] this
   occurred after Siddhartha placed his feet in Asita's hair and Asita
   examined the birthmarks. Suddhodana held a naming ceremony on the fifth
   day, and invited eight [364]Brahmin scholars to read the future. All
   gave a dual prediction that the baby would either become a great king
   or a great holy man.^[365][73] [366]Kondaa, the youngest, and later to
   be the first [367]arhat other than the Buddha, was reputed to be the
   only one who unequivocally predicted that Siddhartha would become a
   [368]Buddha.^[369][74]

   While later tradition and legend characterized Zuddhodana as a
   [370]hereditary monarch, the [371]descendant of the [372]Suryavansha
   (Solar dynasty) of Ikcvku (Pli: Okkka), many scholars think that
   Zuddhodana was the elected chief of a tribal confederacy.

   Early texts suggest that Gautama was not familiar with the dominant
   religious teachings of his time until he left on his religious quest,
   which is said to have been motivated by existential concern for the
   human condition.^[373][75] The state of the Shakya clan was not a
   monarchy, and seems to have been structured either as an
   [374]oligarchy, or as a form of republic.^[375][76] The more
   egalitarian gana-sangha form of government, as a political alternative
   to the strongly hierarchical kingdoms, may have influenced the
   development of the [ramanic [376]Jain and Buddhist [377]sanghas, where
   monarchies tended toward [378]Vedic Brahmanism.^[379][77]

Early life and marriage

   Departure of Prince Siddhartha

   Siddhartha was brought up by his mother's younger sister, [380]Maha
   Pajapati.^[381][78] By tradition, he is said to have been destined by
   birth to the life of a prince, and had three palaces (for seasonal
   occupation) built for him. Although more recent scholarship doubts this
   status, his father, said to be King Zuddhodana, wishing for his son to
   be a great king, is said to have shielded him from religious teachings
   and from knowledge of human [382]suffering.

   When he reached the age of 16, his father reputedly arranged his
   marriage to a cousin of the same age named [383]Ya[odhar (Pli:
   Yasodhar). According to the traditional account,^[[384]which?] she gave
   birth to a son, named [385]Rhula. Siddhartha is said to have spent 29
   years as a prince in [386]Kapilavastu. Although his father ensured that
   Siddhartha was provided with everything he could want or need, Buddhist
   scriptures say that the future Buddha felt that material wealth was not
   life's ultimate goal.^[387][78]

Renunciation and ascetic life

   The Victory of Buddha
   The "Great Departure" of Siddhartha Gautama, surrounded by a [388]halo,
   he is accompanied by numerous guards, [389]maithuna loving couples, and
   [390]devata who have come to pay homage; [391]Gandhara, [392]Kushan
   period
   Prince Siddhartha shaves his hair and becomes an [393]ascetic.
   [394]Borobudur, 8th century

   At the age of 29 Siddhartha left his palace to meet his subjects.
   Despite his father's efforts to hide from him the sick, aged and
   suffering, Siddhartha was said to have seen an old man. When his
   charioteer [395]Channa explained to him that all people grew old, the
   prince went on further trips beyond the palace. On these he encountered
   a [396]diseased man, a decaying [397]corpse, and an [398]ascetic. These
   depressed him, and he initially strove to overcome aging, sickness, and
   death by living the life of an ascetic.^[399][79]

   Accompanied by Channa and riding his horse [400]Kanthaka, Gautama quit
   his palace for the life of a [401]mendicant. It's said that, "the
   horse's hooves were muffled by the gods"^[402][80] to prevent guards
   from knowing of his departure.

   Gautama initially went to [403]Rajagaha and began his ascetic life by
   begging for alms in the street. After King Bimbisara's men recognised
   Siddhartha and the king learned of his quest, Bimbisara offered
   Siddhartha the throne. Siddhartha rejected the offer, but promised to
   visit his kingdom of [404]Magadha first, upon attaining enlightenment.

   He left Rajagaha and practised under two hermit teachers of [405]yogic
   meditation.^[406][81]^[407][82]^[408][83] After mastering the teachings
   of [409]Alara Kalama (Skr. r a Klma), he was asked by Kalama to succeed
   him. However, Gautama felt unsatisfied by the practice, and moved on to
   become a student of [410]yoga with [411]Udaka Ramaputta (Skr. Udraka
   Rmaputra).^[412][84] With him he achieved high levels of meditative
   consciousness, and was again asked to succeed his teacher. But, once
   more, he was not satisfied, and again moved on.^[413][85]

   Siddhartha and a group of five companions led by [414]Kaundinya are
   then said to have set out to take their austerities even further. They
   tried to find enlightenment through deprivation of worldly goods,
   including food, practising [415]self-mortification. After nearly
   starving himself to death by restricting his food intake to around a
   leaf or nut per day, he collapsed in a river while bathing and almost
   drowned. Siddhartha was rescued by a village girl named Sujata and she
   gave him some [416]payasam (a pudding made from milk and [417]jaggery)
   after which Siddhartha got back some energy. Siddhartha began to
   reconsider his path. Then, he remembered a moment in childhood in which
   he had been watching his father start the season's ploughing. He
   attained a concentrated and focused state that was blissful and
   refreshing, the [418]jhna.

Awakening

   The Buddha surrounded by the demons of [419]Mra. [420]Sanskrit
   [421]palm leaf manuscript. [422]Nland, [423]Bihar, [424]India. [425]Pla
   period
   See also: [426]Enlightenment in Buddhism

   According to the early Buddhist texts,^[427][web 14] after realizing
   that meditative [428]dhyana was the right path to awakening, but that
   extreme asceticism didn't work, Gautama discovered what Buddhists call
   the [429]Middle Way^[430][web 14]a path of moderation away from the
   extremes of [431]self-indulgence and self-mortification, or the
   [432]Noble Eightfold Path, as described in the Dhammacakkappavattana
   Sutta, which is regarded as the first discourse of the
   Buddha.^[433][web 14] In a famous incident, after becoming starved and
   weakened, he is said to have accepted milk and [434]rice pudding from a
   village girl named Sujata.^[435][web 15] Such was his emaciated
   appearance that she wrongly believed him to be a spirit that had
   granted her a wish.^[436][web 15]

   Following this incident, Gautama was famously seated under a [437]pipal
   treenow known as the [438]Bodhi treein [439]Bodh Gaya, [440]India, when
   he vowed never to arise until he had found the truth.^[441][86]
   [442]Kaundinya and four other companions, believing that he had
   abandoned his search and become undisciplined, left. After a reputed 49
   days of meditation, at the age of 35, he is said to have attained
   [443]Enlightenment,^[444][86]^[445][web 16] and became known as the
   Buddha or "Awakened One" ("Buddha" is also sometimes translated as "The
   Enlightened One").

   According to some sutras of the Pali canon, at the time of his
   awakening he realized complete insight into the [446]Four Noble Truths,
   thereby attaining [447]liberation from [448]samsara, the endless cycle
   of rebirth, suffering and dying again.^[449][87]^[450][88]^[451][web
   17] According to scholars, this story of the awakening and the stress
   on "liberating insight" is a later development in the Buddhist
   tradition, where the Buddha may have regarded the practice of
   [452]dhyana as leading to [453]Nirvana and
   moksha.^[454][89]^[455][90]^[456][87]^[457][note 10]

   Nirvana is the extinguishing of the "fires" of desire, hatred, and
   ignorance, that keep the cycle of suffering and rebirth
   going.^[458][91] Nirvana is also regarded as the "end of the world", in
   that no personal identity or boundaries of the mind
   remain.^[[459]citation needed] In such a state, a being is said to
   possess the [460]Ten Characteristics, belonging to every
   Buddha.^[[461]citation needed]

   According to a story in the ycana Sutta (Samyutta Nikaya VI.1)  a
   scripture found in the [462]Pli and other [463]canons  immediately
   after his awakening, the Buddha debated whether or not he should teach
   the [464]Dharma to others. He was concerned that humans were so
   overpowered by ignorance, greed and hatred that they could never
   recognise the path, which is subtle, deep and hard to grasp. However,
   in the story, [465]Brahm Sahampati convinced him, arguing that at least
   some will understand it. The Buddha relented, and agreed to teach.

Formation of the sangha

   [466]Dhamek Stupa in Srnth, India, site of the first teaching of the
   Buddha in which he taught the [467]Four Noble Truths to his first five
   disciples

   After his awakening, the Buddha met [468]Taphussa and Bhallika  two
   merchant brothers from the city of [469]Balkh in what is currently
   Afghanistan  who became his first lay disciples. It is said that each
   was given hairs from his head, which are now claimed to be enshrined as
   [470]relics in the [471]Shwe Dagon Temple in [472]Rangoon, [473]Burma.
   The Buddha intended to visit [474]Asita, and his former teachers,
   [475]Alara Kalama and [476]Udaka Ramaputta, to explain his findings,
   but they had already died.

   He then travelled to the [477]Deer Park near [478]Varanasi (Benares) in
   northern India, where he set in motion what Buddhists call the
   [479]Wheel of Dharma by delivering his first sermon to the five
   companions with whom he had sought enlightenment. Together with him,
   they formed the first [480]saEgha: the company of Buddhist monks.

   All five become [481]arahants, and within the first two months, with
   the conversion of [482]Yasa and fifty four of his friends, the number
   of such arahants is said to have grown to 60. The conversion of three
   brothers named Kassapa followed, with their reputed 200, 300 and 500
   disciples, respectively. This swelled the sangha to more than 1,000.

Travels and teaching

   Buddha with his protector [483]Vajrapani, [484]Gandhra, 2nd century CE,
   Ostasiatische Kunst Museum

   For the remaining 45 years of his life, the Buddha is said to have
   traveled in the [485]Gangetic Plain, in what is now [486]Uttar Pradesh,
   [487]Bihar, and southern [488]Nepal, teaching a diverse range of
   people: from nobles to servants, murderers such as [489]Angulimala, and
   cannibals such as Alavaka.^[490][92] Although the Buddha's language
   remains unknown, it's likely that he taught in one or more of a variety
   of closely related Middle Indo-Aryan dialects, of which [491]Pali may
   be a standardization.

   The sangha traveled through the subcontinent, expounding the dharma.
   This continued throughout the year, except during the four months of
   the [492]Vassa rainy season when ascetics of all religions rarely
   traveled. One reason was that it was more difficult to do so without
   causing harm to animal life. At this time of year, the sangha would
   retreat to monasteries, public parks or forests, where people would
   come to them.
   A view of [493]Vulture Peak, where the [494]Atanatiya Sutta was held

   The first vassana was spent at [495]Varanasi when the sangha was
   formed. After this, the Buddha kept a promise to travel to
   [496]Rajagaha, capital of [497]Magadha, to visit King [498]Bimbisara.
   During this visit, [499]Sariputta and [500]Maudgalyayana were converted
   by [501]Assaji, one of the first five disciples, after which they were
   to become the Buddha's two foremost followers. The Buddha spent the
   next three seasons at Veluvana Bamboo Grove monastery in Rajagaha,
   capital of Magadha.

   Upon hearing of his son's awakening, Suddhodana sent, over a period,
   ten delegations to ask him to return to Kapilavastu. On the first nine
   occasions, the delegates failed to deliver the message, and instead
   joined the sangha to become arahants. The tenth delegation, led by
   Kaludayi, a childhood friend of Gautama's (who also became an arahant),
   however, delivered the message.

   Now two years after his awakening, the Buddha agreed to return, and
   made a two-month journey by foot to Kapilavastu, teaching the dharma as
   he went. At his return, the royal palace prepared a midday meal, but
   the sangha was making an alms round in Kapilavastu. Hearing this,
   Suddhodana approached his son, the Buddha, saying:

     "Ours is the warrior lineage of Mahamassata, and not a single
     warrior has gone seeking alms."

   The Buddha is said to have replied:

     "That is not the custom of your royal lineage. But it is the custom
     of my Buddha lineage. Several thousands of Buddhas have gone by
     seeking alms."

   Buddhist texts say that Suddhodana invited the sangha into the palace
   for the meal, followed by a dharma talk. After this he is said to have
   become a [502]sotapanna. During the visit, many members of the royal
   family joined the [503]sangha. The Buddha's cousins [504]Ananda and
   [505]Anuruddha became two of his five chief disciples. At the age of
   seven, his son [506]Rahula also joined, and became one of his ten chief
   disciples. His half-brother [507]Nanda also joined and became an
   arahant.

   Of the Buddha's disciples, Sariputta, [508]Maudgalyayana,
   [509]Mahakasyapa, Ananda and Anuruddha are believed to have been the
   five closest to him. His ten foremost disciples were reputedly
   completed by the quintet of [510]Upali, [511]Subhoti, Rahula,
   [512]Mahakaccana and [513]Punna.

   In the fifth vassana, the Buddha was staying at Mahavana near
   [514]Vesali when he heard news of the impending death of his father. He
   is said to have gone to Suddhodana and taught the dharma, after which
   his father became an arahant.
   The last days of buddha teachings

   The king's death and cremation was to inspire the creation of an order
   of nuns. Buddhist texts record that the Buddha was reluctant to ordain
   women. His foster mother [515]Maha Pajapati, for example, approached
   him, asking to join the sangha, but he refused. Maha Pajapati, however,
   was so intent on the path of awakening that she led a group of royal
   Sakyan and Koliyan ladies, which followed the sangha on a long journey
   to Rajagaha. In time, after Ananda championed their cause, the Buddha
   is said to have reconsidered and, five years after the formation of the
   sangha, agreed to the ordination of women as nuns. He reasoned that
   males and females had an equal capacity for awakening. But he gave
   women additional rules ([516]Vinaya) to follow.

Mahaparinirvana

   The Buddha's entry into [517]Parinirvana. [518]Sanskrit [519]palm leaf
   manuscript. [520]Nland, [521]Bihar, [522]India. [523]Pla period
   Buddha's cremation stupa, [524]Kushinagar (Kushinara).
   The sharing of the [525]relics of the Buddha, ZenyMmitsu-Temple Museum,
   Tokyo

   According to the Mahaparinibbana Sutta of the Pali canon, at the age of
   80, the Buddha announced that he would soon reach Parinirvana, or the
   final deathless state, and abandon his earthly body. After this, the
   Buddha ate his last meal, which he had received as an offering from a
   blacksmith named [526]Cunda. Falling violently ill, Buddha instructed
   his attendant [527]nanda to convince Cunda that the meal eaten at his
   place had nothing to do with his passing and that his meal would be a
   source of the greatest merit as it provided the last meal for a
   Buddha.^[528][web 18] Mettanando and [529]von Hinber argue that the
   Buddha died of [530]mesenteric [531]infarction, a symptom of old age,
   rather than food poisoning.^[532][93]^[533][web 19]

   The precise contents of the Buddha's final meal are not clear, due to
   variant scriptural traditions and ambiguity over the translation of
   certain significant terms; the [534]Theravada tradition generally
   believes that the Buddha was offered some kind of pork, while the
   [535]Mahayana tradition believes that the Buddha consumed some sort of
   truffle or other mushroom. These may reflect the different traditional
   views on [536]Buddhist vegetarianism and the precepts for monks and
   nuns.

   [537]Waley suggests that Theravadins would take suukaramaddava (the
   contents of the Buddha's last meal), which can translate literally as
   pig-soft, to mean "soft flesh of a pig" or "pig's soft-food", that is,
   after [538]Neumann, a soft food favoured by pigs, assumed to be a
   [539]truffle. He argues (also after Neumann) that as "(p)lant names
   tend to be local and dialectical", as there are several plants known to
   have suukara- (pig) as part of their names,^[540][note 11] and as Pali
   Buddhism developed in an area remote from the Buddha's death,
   suukaramaddava could easily have been a type of plant whose local name
   was unknown to those in Pali regions. Specifically, local writers
   writing soon after the Buddha's death knew more about their flora than
   Theravadin commentator [541]Buddhaghosa who lived hundreds of years and
   hundreds of kilometres remote in time and space from the events
   described. Unaware that it may have been a local plant name and with no
   Theravadin prohibition against eating animal flesh, Theravadins would
   not have questioned the Buddha eating meat and interpreted the term
   accordingly.^[542][94]

   According to Buddhist tradition, the Buddha died at Ku[inra
   (present-day [543]Kushinagar, India), which became a pilgrimage
   center.^[544][95] Ananda protested the Buddha's decision to enter
   Parinirvana in the abandoned jungles of [545]Ku[inra of the [546]Malla
   kingdom. The Buddha, however, is said to have reminded Ananda how
   Kushinara was a land once ruled by a righteous wheel-turning king and
   the appropriate place for him to die.^[547][96]

   The Buddha then asked all the attendant [548]Bhikkhus to clarify any
   doubts or questions they had and cleared them all in a way which others
   could not do. They had none. According to Buddhist scriptures, he then
   finally entered Parinirvana. The Buddha's final words are reported to
   have been: "All composite things ([549]SaEkhra) are perishable. Strive
   for your own liberation with diligence" (Pali: 'vayadhamm saEkhr
   appamdena sampdeth'). His body was cremated and the [550]relics were
   placed in monuments or stupas, some of which are believed to have
   survived until the present. For example, The [551]Temple of the Tooth
   or "Dalada Maligawa" in [552]Sri Lanka is the place where what some
   believe to be the [553]relic of the right tooth of Buddha is kept at
   present.
   Life scenes of Buddha, sand stone: Birth, Enlightenment, Descent from
   Heaven, First Sermon, Passing Away, c. 2nd Century CE, [554]Government
   Museum, Mathura.

   According to the Pli historical chronicles of Sri Lanka, the
   [555]D+pavaCsa and [556]MahvaCsa, the coronation of [557]Emperor A[oka
   (Pli: Asoka) is 218 years after the death of the Buddha. According to
   two textual records in Chinese (Ak and p), the coronation of Emperor
   A[oka is 116 years after the death of the Buddha. Therefore, the time
   of Buddha's passing is either 486 BCE according to Theravda record or
   383 BCE according to Mahayana record. However, the actual date
   traditionally accepted as the date of the Buddha's death in Theravda
   countries is 544 or 545 BCE, because the reign of Emperor A[oka was
   traditionally reckoned to be about 60 years earlier than current
   estimates. In Burmese Buddhist tradition, the date of the Buddha's
   death is 13 May 544 BCE.^[558][97] whereas in Thai tradition it is 11
   March 545 BCE.^[559][98]

   At his death, the Buddha is famously believed to have told his
   disciples to follow no leader. [560]Mahakasyapa was chosen by the
   sangha to be the chairman of the [561]First Buddhist Council, with the
   two chief disciples [562]Maudgalyayana and [563]Sariputta having died
   before the Buddha.
   Hair Relics of Buddha on display at [564]Gangaramaya Temple
   ([565]Colombo).

   While in the Buddha's days he was addressed by the very respected
   titles Buddha, Shkyamuni, Shkyasimha, Bhante and Bho, he was known
   after his parinirvana as Arihant, Bhagav/Bhagavat/Bhagwn,
   Mahvira,^[566][99] Jina/Jinendra, Sstr, Sugata, and most popularly in
   scriptures as Tathgata.

Relics

   See also: [567]Zar+ra and [568]Relics associated with Buddha

   After his death, Buddha's cremation relics were divided amongst 8 royal
   families and his disciples; centuries later they would be enshrined by
   [569]King Ashoka into 84,000 stupas.^[570][web 20]^[571][100] Many
   supernatural legends surround the history of alleged relics as they
   accompanied the spread of Buddhism and gave legitimacy to rulers.

Physical characteristics

   Main article: [572]Physical characteristics of the Buddha
   [573]Gandhran depiction of the Buddha from [574]Hadda,
   [575]Afghanistan; Victoria and Albert Museum, [576]London.

   An extensive and colorful physical description of the Buddha has been
   laid down in scriptures. A [577]kshatriya by birth, he had military
   training in his upbringing, and by Shakyan tradition was required to
   pass tests to demonstrate his worthiness as a warrior in order to
   marry.^[[578]citation needed] He had a strong enough body to be noticed
   by one of the kings and was asked to join his army as a
   general.^[[579]citation needed] He is also believed by Buddhists to
   have "the 32 Signs of the Great Man".

   The Brahmin Sonadanda described him as "handsome, good-looking, and
   pleasing to the eye, with a most beautiful complexion. He has a godlike
   form and countenance, he is by no means unattractive." (D, I:115)

   "It is wonderful, truly marvellous, how serene is the good Gotama's
   appearance, how clear and radiant his complexion, just as the golden
   jujube in autumn is clear and radiant, just as a palm-tree fruit just
   loosened from the stalk is clear and radiant, just as an adornment of
   red gold wrought in a crucible by a skilled goldsmith, deftly beaten
   and laid on a yellow-cloth shines, blazes and glitters, even so, the
   good Gotama's senses are calmed, his complexion is clear and radiant."
   (A, I:181)

   A disciple named Vakkali, who later became an arahant, was so obsessed
   by the Buddha's physical presence that the Buddha is said to have felt
   impelled to tell him to desist, and to have reminded him that he should
   know the Buddha through the Dhamma and not through physical
   appearances.

   Although there are no extant representations of the Buddha in human
   form until around the 1st century CE (see [580]Buddhist art),
   descriptions of the physical characteristics of fully enlightened
   buddhas are attributed to the Buddha in the [581]Digha Nikaya's
   LakkhaGa Sutta (D, I:142).^[582][101] In addition, the Buddha's
   physical appearance is described by [583]Yasodhara to their son
   [584]Rahula upon the Buddha's first post-Enlightenment return to his
   former princely palace in the non-canonical Pali devotional hymn,
   Naras+ha Gth ("The Lion of Men").^[585][web 21]

   Among the 32 main characteristics it is mentioned that Buddha has
   [586]blue eyes.^[587][102]

   Recollection of nine virtues attributed to the Buddha is a common
   Buddhist meditation and devotional practice called [588]Buddhnusm[ti.
   The nine virtues are also among the [589]40 Buddhist meditation
   subjects. The nine virtues of the Buddha appear throughout the
   [590]Tipitaka,^[591][web 22] and include:

          Buddho  [592]Awakened
          Sammasambuddho  [593]Perfectly self-awakened
          Vijja-carana-sampano  Endowed with higher knowledge and ideal
          conduct.
          Sugato  Well-gone or Well-spoken.
          Lokavidu  Wise in the knowledge of the [594]many worlds.
          Anuttaro Purisa-damma-sarathi  Unexcelled trainer of untrained
          people.
          Satthadeva-Manussanam  Teacher of [595]gods and humans.
          Bhagavathi  [596]The Blessed one
          Araham  Worthy of homage. An [597]Arahant is "one with taints
          destroyed, who has lived the holy life, done what had to be
          done, laid down the burden, reached the true goal, destroyed the
          fetters of being, and is completely liberated through final
          knowledge."

Teachings

   Lord Buddha at Pandavleni Caves, [598]Nashik.
   Main article: [599]Buddhist philosophy

Use of Brahmanical motifs

   In the Pali Canon, the Buddha uses many [600]Brahmanical devices. For
   example, in [601]Samyutta Nikaya 111, [602]Majjhima Nikaya 92 and
   Vinaya i 246 of the [603]Pali Canon, the Buddha praises the
   [604]Agnihotra as the foremost sacrifice and the [605]Gayatri mantra as
   the foremost meter:

     aggihuttamukh ya svitt+ chandaso mukham.

     Sacrifices have the agnihotra as foremost; of meter the foremost is
     the Svitr+.^[606][103]

Tracing the oldest teachings

   Information of the oldest teachings may be obtained by analysis of the
   oldest texts. One method to obtain information on the oldest core of
   Buddhism is to compare the oldest extant versions of the Theravadin
   [607]Pali Canon and other texts.^[608][note 12] The reliability of
   these sources, and the possibility to draw out a core of oldest
   teachings, is a matter of
   dispute.^[609][106]^[610][107]^[611][108]^[612][109] According to
   Vetter, inconsistencies remain, and other methods must be applied to
   resolve those inconsistencies.^[613][104]^[614][note 13]

   According to Schmithausen, three positions held by scholars of Buddhism
   can be distinguished:^[615][112]
    1. "Stress on the fundamental homogeneity and substantial authenticity
       of at least a considerable part of the Nikayic
       materials;"^[616][note 14]^[617][note 15], from the oldest extant
       texts a common kernel can be drawn out.^[618][113] According to
       Warder, c.q. his publisher: "This kernel of doctrine is presumably
       common Buddhism of the period before the great schisms of the
       fourth and third centuries BC. It may be substantially the Buddhism
       of the Buddha himself, although this cannot be proved: at any rate
       it is a Buddhism presupposed by the schools as existing about a
       hundred years after the parinirvana of the Buddha, and there is no
       evidence to suggest that it was formulated by anyone else than the
       Buddha and his immediate followers"^[619][113] and [620]Richard
       Gombrich.^[621][114] Richard Gombrich: "I have the greatest
       difficulty in accepting that the main edifice is not the work of a
       single genius. By "the main edifice" I mean the collections of the
       main body of sermons, the four Nikyas, and of the main body of
       monastic rules."^[622][109]
    2. "Scepticism with regard to the possibility of retrieving the
       doctrine of earliest Buddhism;"^[623][note 16]^[624][note 17]
    3. "Cautious optimism in this respect."^[625][note 18]

Dhyana and insight

   A core problem in the study of early Buddhism is the relation between
   dhyana and insight.^[626][107]^[627][106]^[628][109] Schmithausen notes
   that the mention of the four noble truths as constituting "liberating
   insight", which is attained after mastering the Rupa Jhanas, is a later
   addition to texts such as Majjhima Nikaya
   36.^[629][110]^[630][106]^[631][107]

Earliest Buddhism

   Main article: [632]Presectarian Buddhism

   According to Tilmann Vetter, the core of earliest Buddhism is the
   practice of [633]dhyna,^[634][118] as a workable alternative to painful
   ascetic practices.^[635][119]^[636][note 19] Bronkhorst agrees that
   Dhyna was a Buddhist invention,^[637][106]^[[638]page needed] whereas
   Norman notes that "the Buddha's way to release [...] was by means of
   meditative practices."^[639][121] Discriminating insight into
   transiency as a separate path to liberation was a later
   development.^[640][122]^[641][123]

   According to the Mahsaccakasutta,^[642][note 20] from the fourth
   [643]jhana the Buddha gained [644]bodhi. Yet, it is not clear what he
   was awakened to.^[645][121]^[646][106]^[[647]page needed] According to
   Schmithausen and Bronkhorst, "liberating insight" is a later addition
   to this text, and reflects a later development and understanding in
   early Buddhism.^[648][110]^[649][106]^[[650]page needed] The mentioning
   of the four truths as constituting "liberating insight" introduces a
   logical problem, since the four truths depict a linear path of
   practice, the knowledge of which is in itself not depicted as being
   liberating:^[651][124]

     [T]hey do not teach that one is released by knowing the four noble
     truths, but by practicing the fourth noble truth, the eightfold
     path, which culminates in right samadhi.^[652][124]

   Although [653]"Nibbna" (Sanskrit: Nirvna) is the common term for the
   desired goal of this practice, many other terms can be found throughout
   the Nikayas, which are not specified.^[654][125]^[655][note 21]

   According to Vetter, the description of the Buddhist path may initially
   have been as simple as the term "the middle way".^[656][126] In time,
   this short description was elaborated, resulting in the description of
   the eightfold path.^[657][126]

   According to both Bronkhorst and Anderson, the [658]four truths became
   a substitution for prajna, or "liberating insight", in the
   suttas^[659][90]^[660][87]^[[661]page needed] in those texts where
   "liberating insight" was preceded by the four jhanas.^[662][127]
   According to Bronkhorst, the four truths may not have been formulated
   in earliest Buddhism, and did not serve in earliest Buddhism as a
   description of "liberating insight".^[663][128] Gotama's teachings may
   have been personal, "adjusted to the need of each person."^[664][127]

   The [665]three marks of existence^[666][note 22] may reflect
   Upanishadic or other influences. K.R. Norman supposes that these terms
   were already in use at the Buddha's time, and were familiar to his
   listeners.^[667][129]

   The [668]Brahma-vihara was in origin probably a brahmanic
   term;^[669][130] but its usage may have been common to the Sramana
   traditions.^[670][106]

Later developments

   In time, "liberating insight" became an essential feature of the
   Buddhist tradition. The following teachings, which are commonly seen as
   essential to Buddhism, are later formulations which form part of the
   explanatory framework of this "liberating
   insight":^[671][107]^[672][106]
     * The [673]Four Noble Truths: that suffering is an ingrained part of
       existence; that the origin of suffering is craving for sensuality,
       acquisition of identity, and fear of annihilation; that suffering
       can be ended; and that following the [674]Noble Eightfold Path is
       the means to accomplish this;
     * The Noble Eightfold Path: right view, right intention, right
       speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right
       mindfulness, and right concentration;
     * [675]Dependent origination: the mind creates suffering as a natural
       product of a complex process.

Other religions

   Buddha depicted as the 9th [676]avatar of god [677]Vishnu in a
   traditional Hindu representation
   Main article: [678]Gautama Buddha in world religions

   Some [679]Hindus regard Gautama as the 9th [680]avatar of
   [681]Vishnu.^[682][note 8]^[683][131] However, Buddha's teachings deny
   the authority of the [684]Vedas and the concepts of
   [685]Brahman-[686]Atman.^[687][132]^[688][133]^[689][134] Consequently
   Buddhism is generally classified as a [690]nstika school (heterodox,
   literally "It is not so"^[691][note 23]) in contrast to the six
   orthodox schools of Hinduism.^[692][137]^[693][138]^[694][139]

   The Buddha is regarded as a [695]prophet by the minority
   [696]Ahmadiyya^[697][web 23] sect of Muslims a sect considered a
   deviant and rejected as [698]apostate by mainstream
   Islam.^[699][140]^[700][141] Some early Chinese Taoist-Buddhists
   thought the Buddha to be a reincarnation of [701]Laozi.^[702][142]

   Disciples of the [703]Cao i religion worship the Buddha as a major
   religious teacher.^[704][143] His image can be found in both their Holy
   See and on the home altar. He is revealed during communication with
   Divine Beings as son of their Supreme Being (God the Father) together
   with other major religious teachers and founders like [705]Jesus,
   [706]Laozi, and [707]Confucius.^[708][144]

   The Christian [709]Saint Josaphat is based on the Buddha. The name
   comes from the Sanskrit [710]Bodhisattva via Arabic Bkdhasaf and
   Georgian Iodasaph.^[711][145] The only story in which St. Josaphat
   appears, Barlaam and Josaphat, is based on the life of the
   Buddha.^[712][146] Josaphat was included in earlier editions of the
   Roman Martyrology (feast day 27 November)  though not in the Roman
   Missal  and in the Eastern Orthodox Church liturgical calendar (26
   August).

   In the ancient [713]Gnostic sect of [714]Manichaeism, the Buddha is
   listed among the prophets who preached the word of God before
   [715]Mani.^[716][147]
